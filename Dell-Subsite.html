Project Overview

Role: UX designer and Developer


Under the UX/UI Bootcamp for McCombs School of Business at UT Austin, we were given a design challenge in which each group represented a mini design studio that worked with a local e-commerce client to make a subsite. We chose our client to be Dell and we chose our subsite to focus on VR/AR technology.

Problem



Problem:

Users have many options as to where to buy electronics, so giving them a stress-free experience when they first purchase a product may inspire them to return for future purchases. Through interviews, surveys, and user testing of Dell’s current website, we found that users are more likely to complete a purchase transaction when the site is clean, simple, and intuitive. Additionally, users are using mobile devices more often to research and buy.


Solution:

Create an intuitive and responsive website focusing on pathways for the three main tasks: creating an account, making a purchase, and handling an error message.

Usability Testing



User Tasks

We also conducted six user testing on Dell’s current website, with people who had varying ranges of experiences with VR/AR. Users were to perform 3 main user tasks: create an account, search for products to purchase, and to go throughout the checkout process.




User Tasks







Key Findings

Through this method, we found out what our users like and disliked about the purchasing process on Dell. The main insight was that users felt overwhelmed by the overload of data on Dell’s site. This caused mental strains, and frustrated the users and didn’t encourage them to continue on checkout.

User Persona


Through our research, we developed our persona. Alex, an ESTJ personality type that thrives on organization and logical task-flows. Alex is looking for a simplistic, informative, and quick purchase experience. Alex’s goal is to find the best deal on AR/VR headsets for gaming and immersive experiences.

User Journey Map




To visualize Alex’s actions and emotions as he goes through the 3 tasks on Dell’s site, we created a Customer Journey Map to outline how he felt during each step, and if there were any opportunities for improvement.

From the journey map, we can see that the biggest design opportunities come Searching/Adding to cart portion. To be exact, our design opportunity is to fix the overload of information that Alex faces when he is viewing and adding his product to the cart. The second area of opportunity for design lies in the checkout section.

Mid-fi Usability Testing



Results

People thought the site was "clean, straightforward, and intuitive”. The layout made sense to our users and followed conventions that they were used to (i.e. having filters, review page at checkout, ratings/reviews, etc).




Opportunities

We saw the most opportunities for improvement in our Cart process. Users were eager to go back to shopping, however they only had the search button and the breadcrumb to go back at our current state. So, we have decided to:
1. add a "continue shopping" button in the cart, along with
2. adding recommended products at the bottom of the cart to solve this issue.


Mid-fi Results

Style Tile

Using our testing results from the Midfi prototypes, we moved forward with our Hifi prototypes and testing. We gathered visual inspiration from websites such as Oculus, EA Games, and Dell’s own sub-brand Alienware. Using the visual styles from those sites as a basis, we made a style tile to establish the design language for our Dell Visor Redesign.



Why NOT stick with Dell's stylesheet?

Our survey showed that most people who use AR/VR have the Oculus headset and primarily use it for gaming purposes. In addition, the Oculus headset utilizes a computer to work with, and Alienware is one of Dell’s most profitable sector according to our Dell resources.
