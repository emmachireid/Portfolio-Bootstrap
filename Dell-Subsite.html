For this project for our UXUI course, we were directed to create a subsite of the company, Dell. In order to create a subsite, we first looked into areas that could use improvement. After some speaking, we decided that AR/VR, along with other necessary devices and accessories will be our main content.

As the co-designer and co-developer, my task was to create the initial mobile design, and then to code half the pages of our product once I finished the initial designs. From past projects, our PM for this project knew where each member excelled, and divided up our tasks accordingly. While this was our first (and last) project where we coded anything, I had the chance of getting hands-on practice with front-end development for the first time.







Problem





Problem: Users have many options as to where to buy electronics, so giving them a stress-free experience when they first purchase a product may inspire them to return for future purchases. Through interviews, surveys, and user testing of Dell’s current website, we found that users are more likely to complete a purchase transaction when the site is clean, simple, and intuitive. Additionally, users are using mobile devices more often to research and buy.



Solution: Create an intuitive and responsive website focusing on pathways for the three main tasks: creating an account, making a purchase, and handling an error message.

User Research: Survey Results




User Research:
Usability Testing Results


We also conducted six user testing on Dell’s current website, with people who had varying ranges of experiences with VR/AR. Users were to perform 3 main user tasks:

1. Create an account
2. Search for products to purchase
3. Go through the checkout process

Through this method, we found out what our users like and disliked about the purchasing process on Dell. The main insight was that users felt overwhelmed by the overload of data on Dell’s site. This caused mental strains, and frustrated the users and didn’t encourage them to continue on checkout.










User Research: Interviews


After creating a script for user interviews, our team members individually proceeded to conduct user interviews.
We focused on asking questions about their online shopping experiences, and about their AR/VR purchases.


Once we finished with interviews, we wrote our transcriptions in one place for easy comparison.






Once we finished writing in our transcriptions for comparison, we silently created an Affinity map.
By doing this, we were able to see clearly that our users had common desires and similar shopping habits when it comes to their AR/VR shopping experience, or online shopping in general.




User Persona


Through our research, we developed our persona. Alex, an ESTJ personality type that thrives on organization and logical task-flows. Alex is looking for a simplistic, informative, and quick purchase experience. Alex’s goal is to find the best deal on AR/VR headsets for gaming and immersive experiences.

AB Testing

1. What we learned:


    For Desktop:
  • Users seemed to like both navigation styles, but preferred the dropdowns more. The Sidebar navigation also takes up content space per page, which made it harder for the users to go through the content.

  • Adoption needs to be its own category on the Primary Navigation, as told from a user who frequently visits and is in a non-profit herself.

  • Landing page per Primary navigation (Adoption landing page, Donation landing Page, Get Involved landing page) is unnecessary and repetitive . It also creates an extra step for the user when navigating throughout the website.

  • By deleting the above "landing page" step, we are able to eliminate the second step in our dropdown navigation. This was something our users was less familiar with.
    For Mobile:
  • Users did not mind either navigation style, as both navigation styles covered the content on the page.

  • Users preferred the dropdown navigation, as it was a clear indicator, yet was a delight in the use.

  • Painpoints: both menu options were high up on the screen, which did not follow the Rule of Thumb. If we bring this menu down, it will be easier for our users to navigate.



2. Our Solution:

For Desktop:
Use the dropdown navigation
and eliminating the second
step navigation.

For Mobile:
Use a slide-up menu,
with a dropdown sub-navigation.
