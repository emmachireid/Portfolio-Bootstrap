UI Design for Google UX Research Recruitment Emails


UI Design challenge for Google's recruitment email templates for UX Research

Overview


Project Overview

Project Type: UI Design Challenge for Employer (non-confidential)
Duration: 1 day


Tools Used

Sketch


Under the UX/UI Bootcamp for McCombs School of Business at UT Austin, we were given a design challenge in which each group represented a mini design studio that worked with a local e-commerce client to make a subsite. We chose our client to be Dell and we chose our subsite to focus on VR/AR technology.







"I would think this is a spam email"

Problem:

Email is bland and lacks in engagement, resulting in response rate lower than 10%.

  • Extremely text-heavy, all text is in the same size
  • All black-and-white
  • Spacing does not separate important messages from unimportant subtext.
  • Not mobile-friendly

Solution:

Create an exciting, responsive email template that catches the reader's eye, while making it obvious who the email came from.

  • Add color and depth to email to catch the reader's eye
  • Use CTA buttons and illustrations for engagement.
  • Separate main message from unimportant text.
  • Keep color consistent throughout email, use different color per email

Lastly, I did a Heuristic Evaluation to rate the usability of Dell’s website.

User Research

After noting our assumptions, and analyzing Dell’s website for usability, it was time to do research to test our hypothesis.
We gathered our user research using 3 different methods:

Surveys


Gather quantitative data on people's online shopping experience on an e-commerce website, along with AR/VR shopping experiences.







Summary:

  • The two main technical obstacles for donating/volunteering was the lack of follow-up, and confusing website navigation.

  • 2. While Donation opportunities were found mostly on social media, people rely on Web Search when finding out about adoption.



User Interviews


Gather general data and insights from AR/VR users, and any methods, key points, and paintpoints of their purchase experience.







Summary:

  • Users were motivated to donate/volunteer when they felt close to the cause, and when they knew what they were getting into.

  • Users do not trust NPOs that have confusing, or untrustworthy websites with long and difficult processes.



User Tests


Gather quantitative data on people's online shopping experience on an e-commerce website, along with AR/VR shopping experiences.







Summary:

  • Users felt overwhelmed by the overload of data.

  • Due to multiple painpoints, users were not encouraged to continue the process.



Afterwards, I Transcribed our notes, wrote down Ethonographic Observations, created a Business Model Canvas , and conducted Affinity Mapping.

User Persona


Through our research, I developed our persona. Alex, an ESTJ personality type that thrives on organization and logical task-flows. Alex is looking for a simplistic, informative, and quick purchase experience. Alex’s goal is to find the best deal on AR/VR headsets for gaming and immersive experiences.

User Journey Map




To visualize Alex’s actions and emotions as he goes through the 3 tasks on Dell’s site, we created a Customer Journey Map to outline how he felt during each step, and if there were any opportunities for improvement.

From the journey map, we can see that the biggest design opportunities come Searching/Adding to cart portion. To be exact, our design opportunity is to fix the overload of information that Alex faces when he is viewing and adding his product to the cart. The second area of opportunity for design lies in the checkout section.

Results

People thought the site was "clean, straightforward, and intuitive”. The layout made sense to our users and followed conventions that they were used to (i.e. having filters, review page at checkout, ratings/reviews, etc).




Opportunities

I saw the most opportunities for improvement in our Cart process. Users were eager to go back to shopping, however they only had the search button and the breadcrumb to go back at our current state. So, we have decided to:
1. add a "continue shopping" button in the cart, along with
2. adding recommended products at the bottom of the cart to solve this issue.

Style Tile


Using our testing results from the Midfi prototypes, we moved forward with our Hifi prototypes and testing. We gathered visual inspiration from websites such as Oculus, EA Games, and Dell’s own sub-brand Alienware. Using the visual styles from those sites as a basis, we made a style tile to establish the design language for our Dell Visor Redesign.



Why NOT stick with Dell's stylesheet?

Our survey showed that most people who use AR/VR have the Oculus headset and primarily use it for gaming purposes. In addition, the Oculus headset utilizes a computer to work with, and Alienware is one of Dell’s most profitable sector according to our Dell resources.
