Responsive redesign for a Non-Profit Organization Website

Under the UX/UI Bootcamp for McCombs School of Business at UT Austin, we were given a design project in our team worked with a stakeholder (Save An Angel) to help them redesign their Non-Profit Organization's website, in hopes to attract donors, volunteers, and fosters.

Emphasize Phase

Stakeholder Meeting
At the start of the project, our team met with our stakeholder to get a better understanding of what their needs, goals, and wants were for the redesign.
  • Save An Angel's main market is women, aged roughly 45-55 years, who are tech-savvy enough to find their way around Facebook.
  • Most people access the website through mobile. Save an Angel's current website is outdated, which is not responsive to screen-size. We also noticed a high inconsistency in style, images, and fonts.
  • Save An Angel’s advertisement is most successful when it pulls at the heartstrings. Imagery evoking compassion and empathy pulls the users in.
  • Most all of their funding comes from individual contributions, and they rely on many volunteers to execute their mission.


Proto-Persona:
To gain an understanding of the users that we are designing for, we developed two Proto-Personas and gathered our assumptions.


Swot Analysis on Competitor:
We also conducted a SWOT analysis with Save An Angel's competitor website (Austin Pets Alive!) to analyze their strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats.


SWOT Analysis on SAA:
Lastly, we conducted a SWOT analysis for Save An Angel for easy comparison.










Analyze / Define
User Research
After noting our assumptions, and analyzing Save an Angel's website for usability, it was time to do research to test our hypothesis. We gathered our user research using 3 different methods.


Survey:
Gather quantitative data on why people choose to volunteer or to give, and what they find as a pain during any of the process.

Survey Findings:
  • The two main technical obstacles for donating/volunteering was the lack of follow-up, and confusing website navigation.
  • While Donation opportunities were found mostly on social media, people rely on Web Search when finding out about adoption




User Interviews:
Gather general data and insights from people who volunteer to understand what users would find important on a NPO website.

Interview Findings:
  • Users were motivated to donate/volunteer when they felt close to the cause, and when they knew what they were getting into.
  • Users do not trust NPOs that have confusing, or untrustworthy websites with long and difficult processes or forms.




User Tests:
Gather quantitative data on people's thoughts while navigating through the current website, pinpointing the opportunities.

User Test Findings:
  • Extremely long form process for volunteering, but there are no information on volunteer duties for users to see.
  • 'Ways to Give' wording is unclear and inconsistent, as users are only given the option to pay. Amazon wishlist is hidden.




Defining




User Insight
Through surveys and user tests, we observed that Save An Angel's current website requires excessive effort for users who are trying to help by donating their time and money for the organization.


Solution:
Redesign Save An Angel’s website to maximize visibility and simplicity of the paths to involvement so that users can quickly gather information and complete their goals with minimal effort and expense, ultimately increasing users’ involvements.

Design / User Test
A few of the team members made Low Fidelity wireframe sketches to brainstorm ideas of potential design layouts. I worked on the desktop/laptop version for our sketches, and another team member worked on the mobile version.

Before working on all the checkout flows, we brought our attention to the navigation styles that made the most sense for our users.







Midfi-Wireframes
After drawing out the low-fi sketches for desktop and mobile, I used Adobe XD to create Mid-fi wireframes and prototyped them for another round of usability testing, where we had our users test out the three tasks we had created flows for (sign-up, searching and adding product to cart, and going through the checkout process).




Mid-fi Purchasing Experience Prototype





Midfi Usability Testing
Results

People thought the site was "clean, straightforward, and intuitive”. The layout made sense to our users and followed conventions that they were used to (i.e. having filters, review page at checkout, ratings/reviews, etc).


Opportunities

I saw the most opportunities for improvement in our Cart process. Users were eager to go back to shopping, however they only had the search button and the breadcrumb to go back at our current state. So to solve this issue, we have decided to:

  • add a "continue shopping" button in the cart
  • adding recommended products at the bottom of the cart




Style Tile
Using our testing results from the Midfi prototypes, we moved forward with our Hifi prototypes and testing. We gathered visual inspiration from websites such as Oculus, EA Games, and Dell’s own sub-brand Alienware. Using the visual styles from those sites as a basis, we made a style tile to establish the design language for our Dell Visor Redesign.




Why did we choose Oculus, EA Games, and Alienware as visual inspiration?

Our survey showed that most people who use AR/VR have the Oculus headset and primarily use it for gaming purposes. In addition, the Oculus headset utilizes a computer to work with, and Alienware is one of Dell’s most profitable sector according to our Dell resources.

Hifi-Prototyping
For the first iteration of the High Fidelity Prototypes, we tested 5 users using the same 3 User Tasks. Each test started off with a 5 second test to gauge the reaction our design had on the user. Next we asked them to complete the 3 User Tasks to see how well they navigated through the prototype with the added graphics, photos, and colors. Lastly the users gave feedback on the original Dell design in comparison to the Hifi prototype pages.






5 Second Test

- "I didn't catch what the website was about, but the photo makes me want to look into it more."


Users were instantly drawn into the large, high-resolution image on our landing page. The color and the tone of the website gave the initial impact that it was a clean, futuristic, fashionable, and eye-catching website.
