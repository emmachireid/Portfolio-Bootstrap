Dell Visor

Responsive Redesign and
Front-end Development of an e-commerce subsite for Dell

Project

Project Overview:

Under the UX/UI Bootcamp for McCombs School of Business at UT Austin, we were given a design challenge in which each group represented a mini design studio that worked with a local e-commerce client to make a subsite.
We chose our client to be Dell and we chose our subsite to focus on VR/AR technology.



Duration:

5 weeks



Project Type:

UX Research, UI Design (Responsive), Front-end Development



Tools Used:

Adobe XD, Google Suite, Trello, Miro, Slack, Illustrator, Photoshop, Visual Studio Code, Github, Bootstrap



Teammates:

5



Role:

UX Designer and Developer



Problem

The Dell Visor, now discontinued, was buried within Dell's online store amongst various other digital products that Dell provides. Users found Dell's current website chaotic, cluttered, and overwhelmed with information.

Our Goal

Design an attractive, user-friendly, intuitive, and responsive subsite for the Dell Visor by focusing on clear pathways with a simplistic, distinctive look and feel while still integrating within the Dell brand.





The Process

Emphasize

Emphasize Phase

Our first step was to study and navigate Dell's current subsite for a better understanding of the potential users of the product would be, and what the products had to offer.
From our evaluations, we created our proto-personas, which we iterated as we conducted research.



Research

User Research

After noting our assumptions, and analyzing Dell’s website for usability, it was time to do research to test our hypothesis. We gathered our user research using 3 different methods; surveys, user interviews, and user tests.



Key Findings:

  • Prioritized Purchasing Criteria for AR/VR: Budget, Functionality, & Reviews (survey)
  • Desktop Priority: Desktop Preference over Mobile for AR/VR (survey + interview)
  • Convenience and Flexibility: 100% prefer online shopping vs brick and mortar (interview)
  • Overload of information on current website (user test)
  • Heavy inconsistencies in design (user test)

Analyze / Define

Defining

We utilized Affinity Mapping to organize and synthesize all the research to identify themes and understand the users' needs. From there, we conducted an Empathy Map to gain a further insight into our user is, to help us understand how they might feel, think, and act.
At the same time, we conducted a business model canvas to define the current problems users were facing, along with our business goals.

Defining our Goal

User Insight

Dell customers need to have a more simple, concise, and streamlined shopping experience because their current experience is confusing and stressful.


Defining the Problem

How might we improve Dell's website so that it is intuitive, effective, and responsive, so customers have a painless online shopping experience?

Problem Statement

We believed that designing an e-commerce subsite for Dell with user-friendly intuitive navigation and site responsiveness will enhance overall user experience.
We also believed, that by using stimulating graphics and photographs, we will be able to inspire our users to stay on Dell’s site to explore further.

Ideation

User Persona:

Through our research, we developed our persona. Alex, an ESTJ personality type that thrives on organization and logical task-flows. Alex is looking for a simplistic, informative, and quick purchase experience as a first time AR/VR buyer. Alex’s goal is to find the best deal on AR/VR headsets for gaming and immersive experiences.





User Journey Map

To visualize Alex’s actions and emotions as he goes through the 3 tasks on Dell’s site, we created a Customer Journey Map to outline how he felt during each step, and if there were any opportunities for improvement.

Design opportunities: Searching/Adding to cart flow, Checkout Flow.







Other Ideation Methods









Design / User Test

Low-fi and Midfi Sketches

A few of the team members made Low Fidelity wireframe sketches to brainstorm ideas of potential design layouts. The Lead UI Designer worked on sketching the Landing and Product Pages, while I designed the Checkout flow. I was in charge of the mobile design.



Mid-fi Purchasing Experience Prototype

After drawing out the low-fi sketches for desktop and mobile, I used Adobe XD to create Mid-fi wireframes and prototyped them for another round of usability testing, where we had our users test out the three tasks we had created flows for.






Midfi Usability Testing

Results

People thought the site was "clean, straightforward, and intuitive”. The layout made sense to our users and followed conventions that they were used to.

Opportunities

  • add a "continue shopping" button in the cart
  • adding recommended products at the bottom of the cart








Style Tile

Using our testing results from the Midfi prototypes, we moved forward with our Hifi prototypes and testing. We gathered visual inspiration from websites such as Oculus, EA Games, and Dell’s own sub-brand Alienware. Using the visual styles from those sites as a basis, we made a style tile to establish the design language for our Dell Visor Redesign.



Why did we choose Oculus, EA Games, and Alienware as visual inspiration?

Our survey showed that most people who use AR/VR have the Oculus headset and primarily use it for gaming purposes. In addition, the Oculus headset utilizes a computer to work with, and Alienware is one of Dell’s most profitable sector according to our Dell resources.


Hifi-Prototyping

For the first iteration of the High Fidelity Prototypes, we tested 5 users using the same 3 User Tasks. Each test started off with a 5 second test to gauge the reaction our design had on the user. Next we asked them to complete the 3 User Tasks to see how well they navigated through the prototype with the added graphics, photos, and colors. Lastly the users gave feedback on the original Dell design in comparison to the Hifi prototype pages.



5 Second Test

- "I didn't catch what the website was about, but the photo makes me want to look into it more."

Users were instantly drawn into the large, high-resolution image on our landing page. The color and the tone of the website gave the initial impact that it was a clean, futuristic, fashionable, and eye-catching website.


Front-end Development









Using HTML, CSS, Bootstrap, and some Javascript, my co-developer and I created the responsive Dell sub-site.
My teammate and I had started building the framework once we finished our low-fis, and iterated as prototyping and testing rounds went by.





Coding Success and Opportunities

Success

  • Creating a Dropdown interaction using Bootstrap
  • Javascript gradient overlays
  • Utilizing tabs for cleaner look on Product page
  • Making a sticky Order Summary box for Cart page
Opportunity: Cart Flow

  • Sticky Order Summary to be responsive
  • Errors should be more visible
  • Real-time cart updates


Opportunity: Product Flow

  • Create a sticky CTA button at bottom
  • Enable extended search bar
  • Enable "Save for Later" option

The Takeaway

As the designer and the co-developer, I learned that communication and collaboration is key. My limited experience in web development was challenged many times during this project, but the teamwork and dedication made everything capable.

Having an understanding, dedicated, communicative team made every step of this journey an enjoyable ride, and I really could have not asked for a better team. Overall, I am proud of what my team and I have accomplished in 5 weeks, from extensive user research, prototyping, to coding our final product.
