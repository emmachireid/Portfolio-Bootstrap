Responsive redesign and
Front-end development of an e-commerce subsite for Dell





Project
Challenge Overview:
Under the UX/UI Bootcamp for McCombs School of Business at UT Austin, we were given a design challenge in which each group represented a mini design studio that worked with a local e-commerce client to make a subsite.
We chose our client to be Dell and we chose our subsite to focus on VR/AR technology.


Duration:
5 weeks


Project Type:
UX Research, UI Design (Responsive), Front-end Development


Tools Used:
Adobe XD


Teammates:
5


Role:
UX Designer and Developer








Problem
Users have many options as to where they can buy electronics, and we want to give them an enjoyable experience so they not only stay on Dell’s website to make an immediate purchase, but return for future purchases.








The Process




Research
Emphasize Phase
At the start of the project, the team met with the stakeholder to get a better understanding of what their needs, goals, and wants were for Dell’s new subsite. Through this initial interview, we identified that the customer wanted to infuse their virtual reality products with products from their gaming sub brand, Alienware. Wanting to keep both new and skilled users in mind, they also wanted the site to be very informative and user-friendly.


Proto-Persona:
To gain an understanding of the users that we are designing for, we developed two Proto-Personas and gathered our assumptions.


Redline:
Next, we moved forward with researching Dell's current website by Redlining to pinpoint areas that worked well and areas that could be improved.


Swot Analysis:
We also conducted a SWOT analysis with the top 3 competitor websites (Best Buy, Apple, and Amazon) to analyze their strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats.


Heuristic Evaluation:
Lastly, we did a Heuristic Evaluation to rate the usability of Dell’s website.










Analyze / Define
User Research
After noting our assumptions, and analyzing Dell’s website for usability, it was time to do research to test our hypothesis. We gathered our user research using 3 different methods.


Survey:
Gather quantitative data on people's online shopping experience on an e-commerce website, along with AR/VR shopping experiences.

Findings:
Prioritized Purchasing Criteria for AR/VR: Budget, Functionality, & Reviews
Website Priority: Desktop Preference over Mobile for AR/VR
Frequent Online Shopping: People buy online often




User Interviews:
Gather general data and insights from AR/VR users, and any methods, key points, and painpoints of their purchase experience.

Findings:
100% of interviewees (sample size:6) prefer online shopping due to convenience, and search flexibility.
100% of interviewees use AR/VR for gaming purposes; 1/6 also used AR/VR for educational purposes.
100% of interviewees prefer shopping on Desktop for electronics, as mobile sites tend to be more busy.




User Tests:
Gather quantitative data on people's online shopping experience on an e-commerce website, along with AR/VR shopping experiences by conducting tasks and checking their navigation.

The Good:

  • Non-text heavy search results page.
  • Custom options prior to purchase.
  • Simple Login.
  • Sticky Cart Summary
The Bad:

  • Overload of information in general.
  • Heavy inconsistencies in design.
  • Extra pages before Adding items to Cart.




Defining




User Insight
Through interviews, surveys, and user testing on Dell’s current website, we discovered that users are more likely to complete a purchase transaction when the site is clean, simple, and intuitive. Additionally, users use both computers and mobile devices to browse and shop online, therefore having a responsive website is important.


How might we...
How might we create subsite of Dell that is fresh and exciting to attract new and old users to Dell’s site, and evoke them to want to explore further on the site?


Solution:
We believed that designing an e-commerce subsite for Dell with user-friendly intuitive navigation and site responsiveness will enhance overall user experience.
We also believed, that by using stimulating graphics and photographs, we will be able to inspire our users to stay on Dell’s site to explore further.

The Three User Tasks:
By focusing on the tasks of 1. creating an account, 2. the purchase process, and 3. quickly dealing with errors, users will be able to effectively make a purchase, in return creating revenue for Dell not only with this transaction, but likely build an investment of returning users back for future purchases.









Ideation
User Persona:
Through our research, we developed our persona. Alex, an ESTJ personality type that thrives on organization and logical task-flows. Alex is looking for a simplistic, informative, and quick purchase experience. Alex’s goal is to find the best deal on AR/VR headsets for gaming and immersive experiences.

User Journey Map
To visualize Alex’s actions and emotions as he goes through the 3 tasks on Dell’s site, we created a Customer Journey Map to outline how he felt during each step, and if there were any opportunities for improvement.

From the journey map, we can see that the biggest design opportunities come Searching/Adding to cart portion. To be exact, our design opportunity was to fix the overload of information that Alex faces when he is viewing and adding his product to the cart. The second area of opportunity for design lies in the checkout section.







Design / User Test
A few of the team members made Low Fidelity wireframe sketches to brainstorm ideas of potential design layouts. The Lead UI Designer worked on sketching the Landing and Product Pages, while I designed the Checkout flow. I was in charge of the mobile design.







Midfi-Wireframes
After drawing out the low-fi sketches for desktop and mobile, I used Adobe XD to create Mid-fi wireframes and prototyped them for another round of usability testing, where we had our users test out the three tasks we had created flows for (sign-up, searching and adding product to cart, and going through the checkout process).




Mid-fi Purchasing Experience Prototype





Midfi Usability Testing
Results

People thought the site was "clean, straightforward, and intuitive”. The layout made sense to our users and followed conventions that they were used to (i.e. having filters, review page at checkout, ratings/reviews, etc).


Opportunities

I saw the most opportunities for improvement in our Cart process. Users were eager to go back to shopping, however they only had the search button and the breadcrumb to go back at our current state. So to solve this issue, we have decided to:

  • add a "continue shopping" button in the cart
  • adding recommended products at the bottom of the cart




Style Tile
Using our testing results from the Midfi prototypes, we moved forward with our Hifi prototypes and testing. We gathered visual inspiration from websites such as Oculus, EA Games, and Dell’s own sub-brand Alienware. Using the visual styles from those sites as a basis, we made a style tile to establish the design language for our Dell Visor Redesign.




Why did we choose Oculus, EA Games, and Alienware as visual inspiration?

Our survey showed that most people who use AR/VR have the Oculus headset and primarily use it for gaming purposes. In addition, the Oculus headset utilizes a computer to work with, and Alienware is one of Dell’s most profitable sector according to our Dell resources.






Prototype /

User Test
Hifi-Prototyping
For the first iteration of the High Fidelity Prototypes, we tested 5 users using the same 3 User Tasks. Each test started off with a 5 second test to gauge the reaction our design had on the user. Next we asked them to complete the 3 User Tasks to see how well they navigated through the prototype with the added graphics, photos, and colors. Lastly the users gave feedback on the original Dell design in comparison to the Hifi prototype pages.


Dell Subsite Hifi Prototypes


5 Second Test

- "I didn't catch what the website was about, but the photo makes me want to look into it more."


Users were instantly drawn into the large, high-resolution image on our landing page. The color and the tone of the website gave the initial impact that it was a clean, futuristic, fashionable, and eye-catching website.
